Lake Eyre South
DESIGNATION
Australia-SA_Lake_Eyre_South
LONGITUDE
136°51.434'E
LATITUDE
29°26.746'S
LOCALITY AND OWNERSHIP
Lake Eyre National Park. Adjacent property: Stuarts Creek Station SA Pastoral Lease 2430. 
ACCESS
Follow Oodnadatta Track south and east 100km from William Creek to the southwestern shore of Lake Eyre South
LOCAL CONTACTS
No entry.
NEAREST SERVICES
William Creek.
RISKS
It is not safe to drive vehicles on the lake bed.
GEOLOGY
The site consists of a traverse from the dissected plain down the slope to the lake bed. The dissection has exposed several sedimentary units. The uppermost is pale yellow fine grained quartz sand up to 2m thick. This is probably the remenants of sand dunes. These are underlain by a layer of pebble conglomerate. The pebbles are composed of red-stained silcreted sandstone. The conglomerate is underlain by another fine grained yellow sand unit of up to 2m thick. This is also probably of aeolean origin. Beneath these is a crumbly grey clay, possibly lacustrine. The lake bed consists of yellow-grey gypsiferous silty sand with halite efflourescence. These sediments are eroded by small streams. The stream beds are composed of pebbly sand and they deposit a series of coalescing low-relief alluvial fans on the lake bed. These fans contain abundant red silcrete pebbles whose distribution becomes sparser with increasing distance from the lake shore. Shallow sheets of brine were observed in the distance.
CLIMATE
The average annual monthly min is 5 deg (July) and the average annual monthly max is 36 deg. The average annual rainfall is 150mm. 
FLORA AND FAUNA
Salt resistant flatbush on lake shore. Small gekko sighted on lake bed. 
HISTORY
Edward John Eyre discovered Lake Eyre in 1840. He believed it to be part of a great horseshoe shaped lake barring any passage to to the north. In 1858 Babbage and Warburton found a way through the lakes and a year later John McDoull Stuart began his series of explorations across the continent. Lake Eyre North was was the site of a world land speed record by Donald Campbell in 1964 when his rocket car, the Bluebird, reached 403.1 miles per hour.
ANALOGUE VALUE
Compared to other analog sites, the Lake Eyre region has little value. The dunes are neither broad enough nor steep enough to represent a challenge to
vehicles or machines. Nor is the lake bed unique, and other sites offer one in addition to other scientifically interesting features. The presence of vehicle tracks
on the lake bed suggest vehicles could operate, but this is risky. The area is logistically remote, a National Park, and does not visually resemble the Martian 
surface.
